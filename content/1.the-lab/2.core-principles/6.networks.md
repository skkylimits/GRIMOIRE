Networks are everywhere, connecting devices like laptops, smartphones, TVs, and IoT devices at home or in businesses, enabling communication and data sharing. They are essential for accessing various information, from personal photos to sensitive data like banking transactions.

> The banking application on your mobile device uses multiple networks to reach your bank. After that, you navigate the bank's network to get to your details.

A network is a system of interconnected devices that enables communication. While many components make up a network, key ones include **routers, switches, firewalls, access points, and hubs**. Two important components to note are:

- **Switches**: Allow multiple devices to communicate within a network.
- **Routers**: Enable communication between different networks, like accessing the internet.

![switch-router](/content/1.the-lab/2.core-principles/7.networks/switch-router.png)

There are two main types of networks:

- **Private Networks**: Require authentication and authorization, typically found in workplaces.
- **Public Networks**: Open to any user, such as the internet.

## How to Connect to Your Network

- **Wired (Ethernet)**: Connects your device to the network using a cable, like in offices.
- **Wireless (Wi-Fi)**: Connects your device to the internet without cables, like at home or cafes.
- **Bluetooth**: Used for small devices like headphones or smartwatches, connects them without wires but only over short distances.

### Client-Server Topology

In a **client-server model**, the **client** is a device or app that asks for something, like your phone or computer. The **server** is the computer that handles the request and sends back the information.

![client-server](/content/1.the-lab/2.core-principles/7.networks/client-server.png)

For example:

- When you watch a movie on a streaming app, your phone is the **client**, and the streaming server sends you the movie.
- When you use a browser to access websites, your browser (client) asks the web server for content, and the server sends it back to you.

Data moves around a network when two or more devices share information. Networks use communication protocols like TCP/IP to make sure data gets to the right place.

- TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) helps devices connect and send data to each other.

- IP (Internet Protocol) ensures that data finds the right path through the network.

This allows all kinds of data, like messages, pictures, and even movies, to move around networks every second of the day.

### The Datagram or Packet

When you send or receive data, it’s broken into small pieces called packets. For example, when streaming a movie, the video is split into many packets. Your device waits for enough packets before it can start playing. If the packets are delayed, the movie might look or sound weird.

### IP Addresses

IP addresses are like the address on an envelope. When you send data over the internet, it gets broken into packets, and each packet has an address telling it where to go and where it came from. This way, devices know where to send the information.

There are two types of IP addresses:

- IPv4: The most common one looks like this: 127.100.0.1.
- IPv6

### DNS

DNS is like a phone book for the internet. Every website has its own IP address, but it’s easier to remember names like microsoft.com. When you type a website name, DNS helps your browser find the correct IP address to get you to the right site, just like looking up a phone number in a directory.

![dns-lookup-table](/content/1.the-lab/2.core-principles/7.networks/dns-lookup-table.png)

When your device connects to the internet, it asks a local DNS server to find the website you're looking for. If that server can't find the site, it asks other DNS servers. If no one can find it or if there’s a delay, you'll get an error like "DNS server not responding

### Routing

When a packet is ready, if the destination IP address is within your network, it’s like delivering a letter by hand to a nearby friend—no need for a postal service. But if the destination is outside your network, the packet has to go through a router. The router is like a postal service that finds the best route to send the packet to the correct network, much like sending a letter to a friend in another city.

## Network Attacks

::callout

Networks are targets for cybercriminals because they store sensitive data. Common attacks include:

- **Man-in-the-middle**: Hackers intercept data being sent between devices. Like wiretapping.
- **DDoS**: Attackers overload a network or service with too many requests, causing it to crash.
- **DNS poisoning**: A DNS attack looks to exploit weaknesses in the DNS server because they're designed for efficiency and usability, and not with security in mind. A common DNS attack is DNS poisoning. This is where the attacker changes the IP addresses in the DNS lookup tables to divert traffic from a legitimate site to a bad site that might contain malicious links or other malware.
- **Wireless attacks**:
	- **Wardriving**, where they search for vulnerable networks from a vehicle
	- **Spoofing trusted Wi-Fi hotspots** to trick users into connecting and steal their data.
- **Bluetooth attacks**: Criminal needs to be within range of your device – but it's still a valid attack vector. A Bluejacking attack is where a criminal sends unsolicited messages to any Bluetooth-enabled device that's within range of their own. Bluejacking is similar to when someone rings your doorbell and then runs away before you can answer. It's mostly an annoyance.

These attacks damage the safety and privacy of data on networks.

## Network Protection

Networks help us use the internet, play games, talk to friends, and do schoolwork. But bad guys (called hackers) want to mess things up or steal stuff. Here’s how to protect your network like a superhero:

- **Firewall**: A firewall is typically the frontline of defense in your network. It's a device that sits between the internet and your network. Like a security guard at the door. It checks everything going in and out of the network and only lets in the good stuff.

- **Antivirus**: Like a robot that fights off computer germs. It looks for bad files and gets rid of them before they can cause trouble.

- **Network Access Control (NAC)**: While a firewall keeps unwanted devices from accessing your network, you still need to control the ones that you do want to use it. Makes sure only the right people and devices can get on the network. It’s like a guest list at a party.

- **Network Segmentation**: This splits the network into different rooms. If a bad guy gets into one room, he can’t get into all the others. It keeps important stuff extra safe.

- **VPN (Virtual Private Network)**: This serves as a dedicated and secure connection, between a device and a server, across the internet. A secret tunnel that hides what you're doing online. If you're using public Wi-Fi, like at a coffee shop, the VPN keeps everything private.

- **Wi-Fi Encryption**: This scrambles your internet traffic like a secret code. Even if someone is listening, they won’t understand what you're saying. Wi-Fi Protected Access 2 (WPA2) is the most commonly used Wi-Fi encryption method. It uses the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) to secure the connection.
