The words cryptography and encryption may conjure visions of spies and clandestine operations, or of hackers sat in windowless rooms. Yet much of today's modern online world wouldn't be possible without what is meant by those two words.

> Cryptography and encryption are the cornerstones of any good cybersecurity solution. For example, they help to keep your emails safe from prying eyes and protect online payments.

## Cryptography

Cryptography is the art of obscuring the meaning of a message to everyone but the intended recipient.

The desire to keep secrets has been with us since we first learned to communicate. As we developed new methods and ways of communicating over the centuries, the need to share secrets with friends and allies grew.

> Cryptography, derived from the Greek word "Kryptos, which means hidden or secret, is the application of secure communication in any form between a sender and a recipient. Typically, cryptography is used to obscure the meaning of a written message, but it can also be applied to images.

### Key Concepts

As with all systems, cryptography has its own language of terms and phrases. wo of the important ones are plaintext and ciphertext.

- **Plaintext:** The original, unencrypted message or data.
- **Ciphertext:** The encrypted message that hides the original content.

## Encryption

> The methods used to encrypt a message have evolved over thousands of years, from swapping one letter for another to more elaborate mechanical devices like the Enigma machine.

Encryption now takes place in the digital world. It uses computers and mathematics to combine large random prime numbers to create keys that are used in both symmetric and asymmetric encryption.

- **Encryption:** Is the mechanism by which plaintext messages are turned into unreadable ciphertext
- **Decryption :** Is the mechanism by which the recipient of a ciphertext message can turn it back into readable plaintext.

To facilitate the encryption and decryption process, you need to use a secret encryption key. This key is much like the one you’d use to open your car, or the door to your house. Encryption keys come in two flavors:

- **Symmetric keys**
- **Asymmetric keys**

### Symmetric keys

Symmetric key encryption uses the same key to lock and unlock a message. Imagine you and a friend both have a secret code to open a special box. To keep your messages safe, you both need to have the same secret code, and you have to make sure no one else finds it.

![symmetric-encryption](/content/1.the-lab/2.core-principles/symmetric-encryption.png)

If there are just a few people who need to use the code, it's easy to share. But if you have a big group, like a company with 100 people, each person needs a unique code for everyone else, which adds up to a lot of codes—4,950 in total! For a huge group, like a government-based organization with 1,000 people, you'd need 499,500 codes!

Managing and sharing all these secret codes can be really tricky and expensive, especially as the group gets bigger.

### Asymmetric or Public-Key Encryption

Asymmetric encryption, developed in the 1970s, makes sharing secret keys easier. Instead of one key, it uses a pair: a public key, which anyone can see, and a private key, which stays secret with its owner.

Here's how it works:

- You can share your public key with anyone.
- To send a secure message, you use the recipient's public key to encrypt it.
- Only the recipient's private key can decrypt and read the message.

This system simplifies key sharing and avoids the huge number of keys needed in symmetric encryption, where everyone needs a separate key for each person. For a large organization, asymmetric encryption saves a lot of keys and effort.

![key-pair-generation](/content/1.the-lab/2.core-principles/key-pair-generation.png)

### Different Types of Encryptions

Here are some types of encryption, which help keep our information safe:

1. **DES (Data Encryption Standard)**: An early way to keep data safe by scrambling it. Later, a stronger version called **Triple-DES** was used, which applies DES three times for extra security.

2. **AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)**: This is the new and improved way to scramble data. It replaced DES and is used everywhere today because it's really strong.

3. **RSA**: A method that uses two keys for encrypting and decrypting data. It's one of the first methods for public-key encryption and is still widely used.

These encryption methods help protect our messages and information from being read by anyone except the intended person.

## Where is Encryption Used?

Encryption is everywhere in our daily lives, keeping our information safe. Here's where you might find it:

1. **Web Browsing**: When you visit a website with "https://" or see a padlock icon in the address bar, encryption is protecting your data. This keeps your personal info safe when shopping online or checking your bank account.

2. **Device Encryption**: Tools like Windows BitLocker encrypt your computer’s hard drive or external drives, so your files stay secure even if your device is lost or stolen.

3. **Messaging Apps**: Many messaging apps use encryption to keep your chats private and secure.

4. **Mobile Communications**: Encryption helps keep your phone calls and messages safe and ensures your phone connects securely to cell towers.

Encryption helps protect your personal information and ensures your digital communications remain private.

### Hashing Function

This is done using a mathematical algorithm known as a hashing function.

Hashing uses an algorithm, also known as a hashing function, to convert the original text to a unique fixed-length value. This is called a hash value. Each time the same text is hashed using the same algorithm, the same hash value is produced. That hash can then be used as a unique identifier of its associated data.

![hashing-function](/core-principles/hashing-function.png)

#### The Irreversible Process with Unique Integrity

A key characteristic of hashing is that even a small change in the original data results in a completely different hash value.

Hashing is different to encryption in that it doesn't use keys, and the hashed value can’t decrypt back to the original.

#### Types of Hasing Functions

There are many different types of hash functions. One that's common, and you may hear in discussions with security professionals, is the Secure Hash Algorithm (SHA). SHA is a family of hash algorithms that each work differently. The details are beyond the scope of this content, but one of the more commonly used SHAs is SHA-256, which produces a hash value that is 256 bits long.

### Digital Signature

A digital signature is a cryptographic technique used to validate the authenticity and integrity of a digital document. It ensures that the document has not been tampered with and verifies the identity of the person who signed it.

#### How Does A It Work?

> Monica signs a document by encrypting its time-stamped hash with her private key and sends it with her public key to Victoria. Victoria decrypts the hash and compares it with a new hash from the document; if they match, the document is verified as authentic.

### Digital Certificates

Cryptography is widely used today, ensuring message confidentiality through encryption and verifying message integrity with hashing in digital signatures. Digital certificates add another security layer by verifying the identity of individuals or entities in digital communications. Issued by a Certificate Authority (CA), these certificates function like passports, confirming the subject's identity and public key. To obtain one, a requestor submits a certification request to a CA, which reviews and issues the certificate.

Digital certificates typically last a year.

Digital certificates are commonly seen in HTTPS websites, indicated by a padlock icon in the browser.

#### Why do we need them?

> Quincy and Monica want to send secret messages to each other. They use asymmetric encryption, where each has a public key (which they can share) and a private key (which they keep secret). Quincy uses Monica’s public key to send her a secure message, and only Monica can unlock it with her private key.

But there's a problem: how does Quincy know that Monica's public key really belongs to her? And how does Monica know that Quincy is really the sender?

This is where digital certificates help. A certificate, issued by a trusted authority, links a public key to a person’s identity. So, when Quincy uses Monica's certificate, he's sure it's really from her. If Quincy also has a certificate, he can sign his message, proving to Monica that it really came from him and hasn't been altered.
