Software is a set of coded instructions that tell a computer or device what to do. It runs on top of the device's hardware and comes in two main types:

- **System software** – Helps the device run and supports other programs.
- **Application software** – Helps users do specific tasks, like writing or browsing.

### System software

System software is what starts when you turn on your device. It controls hardware like the keyboard, mouse, and network, and supports apps so they run properly.

-  Runs in the background
- Works on its own
- Manages system functions

Examples: operating systems, antivirus, and firewalls.
Even system software can be attacked by cybercriminals.

### Application software

Application software (or apps) are programs made for specific tasks like writing documents, sending emails, or browsing the web.

- Made for specific jobs like messaging, editing, or gaming
- Users interact with it directly
- Needs system software to run
- Often needs to be installed or managed by the user

> Apps are getting smarter—they can track your location and collect data like where you go, how long you stay, and your search history, even when you're not using them. Collected data is often shared with other apps—like your search history being used by social media to show targeted ads.

**Examples**: Word processors, browsers, email apps, games, etc.

Applications are essential to daily life and make tasks easier, but they also collect vast personal data. Cybercriminals target these apps to exploit weaknesses, so understanding threats and securing data is crucial for everyone.

### Untrusted Sources

Downloading apps is easier than ever, but untrusted sources often lack verification, allowing cybercriminals to disguise malicious apps as legitimate ones—putting users at risk of cyberattacks.

### Inherent Vulnerabilities

Even though developers work to secure their apps, no app is 100% safe—cybercriminals often exploit vulnerabilities like open-source flaws or zero-day bugs.

- **Open-source vulnerabilities**
  Open-source libraries help developers solve common problems efficiently, but they’re also accessible to cybercriminals. Since vulnerabilities are public, developers must keep libraries up to date to avoid potential attacks.

- **Zero-day vulnerabilities**
  Cybercriminals search apps for hidden flaws, known as zero-day vulnerabilities—issues unknown to the developer and unpatched. These are exploited in secret, like in a banking app, where attackers could quietly steal user data or money before a fix is made.

- **Cookie-based attacks**
  Cookies are small text files that store data like your login info or recent activity to improve your browsing experience. However, they can be exploited—such as in a session replay attack, where a cybercriminal intercepts your cookie data to impersonate you on a website.

- **Typosquatting**
  Typosquatting is a browser-based attack where cybercriminals create fake websites using misspelled versions of popular domains. These sites mimic the real ones to trick users into sharing personal information, leading to cyberattacks.

## Application Protection

Applications are essential to daily life, and while developers work to keep them secure, cybercriminals exploit vulnerabilities. It's crucial for individuals and organizations to take steps to protect their apps.

- **Patching Promptly**: Operating systems and apps regularly issue updates to fix security vulnerabilities. Cybercriminals target these weaknesses to deploy malware, compromising data or controlling applications until the next patch. Ensure all apps on your device are up-to-date for security.

- **Application Configuration**: Most apps balance security and usability, with default settings for ease of use, such as default accounts and passwords. Cybercriminals exploit these vulnerabilities. Change default passwords and configurations to protect your data and app security.

- **Privacy Settings**: Apps track your activity for improvement and advertising. Customize privacy settings to control what data is collected, such as stopping a map app from tracking routes.

- **Cookies**: Browsers use cookies to store details like searches and personal data, which cybercriminals can exploit. Regularly clean up unused cookies or use private browsing (incognito) for better security, as it automatically deletes cookies and history when the window is closed.

- **Using Verified Applications**: In the past, buying apps meant going to a store and installing them from a CD-ROM, which was safer. Today, while we can easily download apps online, cybercriminals may offer cheaper, compromised versions with malware. Always download apps from verified and trusted stores to avoid these risks.
