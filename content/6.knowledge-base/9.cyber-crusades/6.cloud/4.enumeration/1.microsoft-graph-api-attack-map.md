## **1. Users**

Understanding who the users are and what they own or have access to is a key element in attack path enumeration.

**Why?**
- Users owning applications with **high-level roles** (e.g., **Global Admin**) could escalate privileges significantly.
- Mapping users to applications can help identify **potentially compromised users** with elevated privileges.

**What resources exist?**
- **Enumerate Users:** Use the `Get-MgUser` cmdlet to get information about all users.
	```powershell
	Get-MgUser -All
	```

- **Find Specific Users:** Search for a specific user by email or ID.
	```powershell
	Get-MgUser -UserId test@domain.com
	```

- **User Attributes:** Get details about user attributes that may indicate additional access.
	```powershell
	Get-MgUser -UserId test@domain.com | Format-List *
	```

- **Synced vs Cloud-Only Users:** Identify users synced from on-prem AD vs cloud-only users.
	```powershell
	Get-MgUser -All | Where-Object {$_.OnPremisesSecurityIdentifier -ne $null}  # Synced users
	Get-MgUser -All | Where-Object {$_.OnPremisesSecurityIdentifier -eq $null}  # Cloud-only users
	```

- **Objects Created or Owned by Users:** Determine what objects are created or owned by the users, such as applications, devices, etc.
	```powershell
	Get-MgUserCreatedObject -UserId test@domain.com
	```

## **2. Groups**

Next, enumerate the groups that users belong to, as group memberships are often leveraged for privilege escalation.

**Why?**
- Being a member of a group with high privileges (e.g., **Global Admin**) can grant an attacker escalated privileges.
- Identifying **admin groups** or groups with high privileges can help attackers pivot or escalate attacks.

**What groups exist?**
- **List All Groups:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgGroup -All
	```

- **Dynamic & Synced Groups:** Look for groups with dynamic membership or those synced from on-prem AD.
	```powershell
	Get-MgGroup | Where-Object {$_.GroupTypes -eq 'DynamicMembership'}
	Get-MgGroup -All | Where-Object {$_.OnPremisesSecurityIdentifier -ne $null}  # Synced groups
	```

- **Find Groups with Specific Roles:** You can identify groups tied to specific roles such as **Global Admin**.
	```powershell
	Get-MgGroup -All | Where-Object {$_.DisplayName -match "admin"}
	```

- **Get Members of a Group:** Use this command to enumerate group memberships.
	```powershell
	Get-MgGroupMember -GroupId <GroupId>
	```

## **3. Roles**

Azure roles can provide users or applications access to sensitive resources. Attackers will target these roles to escalate their privileges.

**Why?**
    - Attackers focus on identifying users with elevated roles (like **Global Admin**) to escalate privileges and gain control of the environment.

**What roles exist and who has them?**
- **List All Roles:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgDirectoryRoleTemplate
	```

- **Enumerate Directory Roles Assigned to Users:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgDirectoryRole | Where-Object {$_.DisplayName -eq 'Global Administrator'}
	```

- **Role Members:** Find which users are assigned specific roles.
	```powershell
	Get-MgDirectoryRoleMember -DirectoryRoleId $RoleId | Select-Object DisplayName, Id
	```

## **4. Devices**

Devices, especially those Azure AD-joined, are critical for lateral movement. Owning a device as a local admin can provide attackers with additional capabilities.

**What devices exist and who owns them?**
- **Enumerate Devices:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgDevice -All
	```

- **Device Owners:** Identify device owners, as they typically have local admin privileges on those devices.
	```powershell
	Get-MgDeviceRegisteredOwner -DeviceId <DeviceId>
	```

- **List Devices Owned by a Specific User:**
	```powershell
	(Get-MgUserOwnedDevice -UserId user@domain.com).AdditionalProperties
	```

**Why This Matters:**
- If an attacker compromises a user, they can potentially gain **local admin access** on all devices owned by that user, allowing lateral movement.
- Devices can also help escalate access within the environment (e.g., **Azure AD-joined** devices automatically give local admin access).

## **5. Applications & Service Principals**

Applications in Azure are not just for automating processes—they can also grant access to critical resources. Service principals, linked to applications, can have roles and permissions that may give attackers significant control.

**Why?**
- Owning an application can give attackers **full control** over its permissions, including adding **client secrets** that bypass MFA.
- **Service principals** tied to high-privileged apps (like **Global Admin**) can grant attackers **admin access** to Azure resources.

**What applications exist and what permissions do they have?**
- **List All Applications (App Registrations):**
	```powershell
	Get-MgApplication -All
	```

- **List Service Principals (Enterprise Applications):**
	```powershell
	Get-MgServicePrincipal -All
	```

- **Find Applications by Display Name:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgApplication -All | Where-Object {$_.DisplayName -match "app"}
	```

- **Find Service Principals with a Password or Key:**
	```powershell
	Get-MgServicePrincipal -All | Where-Object {$_.KeyCredentials -ne $null}
	```

- **Check Application Owners:**
	```powershell
	(Get-MgApplicationOwner -ApplicationId <AppId>).AdditionalProperties.userPrincipalName
	```
