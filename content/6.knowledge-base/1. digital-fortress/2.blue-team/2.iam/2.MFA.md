**Definition**: A security process requiring users to provide two or more verification factors to gain access to an application or system, enhancing the protection beyond just a password.

## **Core Elements**

1. **Authentication Factors**:

   - **Something You Know** (e.g., password or PIN):
     - **Definition**: Involves passwords or PINs that only the user is supposed to know. This is the first line of defense and often the weakest link if not combined with additional factors.
     - **Storage**: Typically stored in a **hashed and encrypted format** within **Active Directory (AD)** for on-premises environments or in **Azure Active Directory (Azure AD)** for cloud environments.
   - **Something You Have** (e.g., trusted device, hardware key):
     - **Definition**: Refers to physical items or digital tokens that the user possesses, such as a hardware security key or a mobile phone used to receive one-time passcodes (OTPs). These items are difficult to duplicate, adding an extra layer of security.
     - **Storage**: Device registrations and associated tokens are often stored in **Active Directory (AD)** or managed by cloud-based identity providers like **Azure AD**, **Okta**, or **Ping Identity**.
   - **Something You Are** (e.g., biometrics):
     - **Definition**: Involves biometric data such as fingerprints, facial recognition, or iris scans. These are unique to each individual and are hard to replicate, providing a strong layer of security based on physical traits.
     - **Storage**: **Biometric data** is generally stored locally on the device (e.g., in a secure enclave on a smartphone) and is not sent to external servers. In some cloud environments, **Azure AD** may store **hashes** of biometric data securely if the organization uses cloud-based authentication services.

2. **Implementation Methods**:

   - **Integration Approaches**: MFA can be incorporated into various systems using different methods:
     - **SMS-Based Codes**: Users receive a one-time passcode via SMS, which must be entered in addition to their password. While easy to implement, it may be vulnerable to interception or SIM-swapping attacks.
     - **Authentication Apps**: Apps like Google Authenticator or Authy generate time-based one-time passcodes (TOTPs) that users enter alongside their password. These apps are more secure than SMS and reduce the risk of interception.
     - **Hardware Tokens**: Physical devices such as USB keys that generate or store authentication codes. These tokens offer robust security by requiring the user to have the physical device to complete the login process.
     - **Biometric Authentication**: Uses biometric data like fingerprints or facial recognition to verify identity. This method provides high security due to the uniqueness of biometric traits but requires specialized hardware.

3. **Security Benefits**:
   - **Enhanced Protection**: By requiring multiple forms of verification, MFA significantly reduces the risk of unauthorized access. Even if an attacker compromises one factor (like a password), they would still need the additional factor(s) to gain access.
   - **Mitigation of Common Attacks**: MFA helps protect against various types of attacks, including phishing, credential stuffing, and brute-force attacks. By introducing multiple verification steps, MFA ensures that stolen or guessed passwords alone are insufficient to breach an account.
   - **Reduced Impact of Compromised Credentials**: In the event that one authentication factor is compromised, the additional factors act as barriers, preventing attackers from easily accessing systems or sensitive information.
