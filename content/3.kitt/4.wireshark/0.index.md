Wireshark is a **network packet analyzer**. A **network packet analyzer** presents captured packet data in as much detail as possible.

You could think of a **network packet analyzer** as a measuring device for examining what’s happening inside a network cable, just like an electrician uses a voltmeter for examining what’s happening inside an electric cable (but at a higher level, of course).

- **Network administrators** use it to troubleshoot network problems
- **Network security engineers** use it to examine security problems
- **QA engineers** use it to verify network applications
- **Developers** use it to debug protocol implementations
- People use it to **learn** network protocol internals

## UI

### 🧭 **Menu Bar**

> The menu bar acts as the **Central Command Center** for all the tools and options within Wireshark.
> Think of it like the control panel of a spaceship, allowing you to access different functionalities—neatly organized.

It’s much like navigating through the applications menu on your computer.

![menu-bar](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/menu-bar.png)

### 🧰 **Toolbar**

> The toolbar functions as a **quick access toolkit**.
> It houses **commonly used tools**, making it easy to find what you need without searching through menus.

For instance, you can **start, stop, or restart captures** with just a click.
There are also options for automatically **following live captures** and more.

![toolbar](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/toolbar.png)

### 🔎 **Filter Bar**

> The filter bar is where you type in **specific criteria** for what you want to see.
> Wireshark will then **filter the captured packets accordingly**.
> It's like a powerful search tool that helps you **focus on the traffic that matters**.

🧠 Example:
Type `http` into the filter box and Wireshark shows only HTTP-related packets.

**Filters can be based on:**

* IP addresses
* Protocols
* Ports
* Packet types
  ...and more.

![filter-bar](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/filter-bar.png)

### 📜 **Packet List Pane**

> This is your **window into the network**.
> It displays a **comprehensive list of packets** as they travel through the network—like watching a live stream of data.
>
> Selecting a packet here brings up deeper details in the **Packet Details Pane**.

![package-list-pane](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/package-list-pane.png)
### 🔍 **Packet Details Pane**

> When you select a packet, this pane becomes your **treasure trove of info**.
> Think of it like **opening an envelope** to reveal the contents.

**You’ll see:**

* Source and destination
* Protocols in use
* Layer-by-layer breakdowns of the packet

![package-details-pane](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/package-details-pane.png)

### 💾 **Bytes Pane (Hex View)**

> This reveals the **actual data inside packets**.
> You’ll see **hexadecimal values** and their corresponding ASCII.

It’s like **decoding a secret message**—a raw, unfiltered view of the packet.

![hex-view](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/hex-view.png)

### 🧮 **Status Bar**

> The status bar is like your **assistant providing real-time updates**.
> It gives you:

* Total packets captured
* Capture duration
* Status of the current capture
* Error or warning indicators

![status-bar](/content/3.kitt/4.wireshark/0.index/status-bar.png)

### 📉 **What if you see no packets?**

Sometimes the packet list is **empty**, and it can be frustrating. A few reasons this may happen:

1. **No network traffic** is occurring at that moment.

   > Like trying to catch a bus when none are running.
2. The traffic is **not meant for your device**.

   > Like eavesdropping on a conversation too far away.
3. **Promiscuous mode** is not enabled.

   > Without it, Wireshark won’t capture traffic not addressed to your machine.
