## What Are Devices?

What are devices?

When you hear about a "device," what's the first thing that comes to mind? You'll probably think about the ones you're familiar with, such as your phone, laptop, or tablet. Devices encompass a lot more than this. For example:

- USB drives.
- Any device connected to your home network, including always-on home assistance devices, printers, TVs, appliances, door cameras, printers, and more.
- Car dashboards, including the navigation system and voice control.
- Wi-Fi hotspots.

> In the context of cybersecurity, anything you can touch or interact with, and that can also connect to something else, is considered to be a device

**What Does Your Device Know About You?**
We use devices all the time — sometimes we don’t even notice! Phones, laptops, smartwatches, and even stuff like your car or smart fridge are all devices. And guess what? They know a lot about you.

### **Devices are everywhere**

- At home: your phone, tablet, smart TV, speaker, or even your fridge might be connected.
- In the car: navigation systems and Bluetooth help you get places and play music.
- At school or work: laptops, phones, printers, and USB drives all connect to networks.

### **They collect info**

Devices collect all kinds of things like:

- Where you are (location)
- What websites you visit
- What you search for online
- What apps you use and for how long

> Ever wonder why you see an ad for something you just talked about? It’s probably because your device is always listening and learning.

### **Some devices work without you doing anything**

Your phone might connect to Wi-Fi by itself. Your smartwatch may be sending data in the background. That’s why you should know what’s connected and how it’s working.

### **Cybercriminals want your info**

Whether you use the device for work or personal business—or both—the accessible information is often sensitive and private. Cybercriminals know this and try to compromise devices as a means to access data

### Threat Vectors

Yep, devices help us do all kinds of cool stuff, but they can also be used by bad guys (cybercriminals!) to cause trouble. We call these "threat vectors" — which just means ways a hacker can sneak in.

- **Phones, laptops, tablets**: Bad apps can put *malware* on your device. It’s like a spy that steals your stuff or messes it up.
- **USB drives**: Hackers can load *ransomware* on them, which locks your files and asks for money to unlock them.
- **Smart speakers/home assistants**: Fake apps can let hackers *listen in* or *spy* on you. They might even break into your other devices at home!

### Vulnerabilities

- **Out-of-date devices**: If your device doesn’t have the latest updates or uses weak passwords, hackers can break in—especially on public Wi-Fi like at airports.
- **Malware installs**: Once in, attackers can add sneaky programs like *back doors* or *botnets* that stay hidden—even after updates. They spread when you connect to home or work networks.
- **Jailbreaking**: Some people *jailbreak* their devices to customize them. But this removes safety locks and makes it easy for hackers to trick them with fake apps or bad advice.
- **Any device**: If it’s not secure, it can be used by cybercriminals—even ones you don’t expect.

To protect your devices from threats, here are a few important ways to keep them safe:

1. **Device Hardening**:
   - Keep your devices updated with the latest security patches.
   - Turn off devices you aren’t using.
   - Use features like PIN codes or facial recognition to lock your devices.
   - Using [hardening strategies](https://github.com/HotCakeX/Harden-Windows-Security).

2. **Encryption**:
   - Encryption turns the data on your device into unreadable information. Only the person with the right key or password can unlock it. This helps keep your information safe.

3. **Limit App Access**:
   - Always lock your device when you're not using it, especially if you're stepping away from your phone or computer.
   - This way, no one can access your apps and sensitive data when you're not around.
