## **History**

In 2011, **Lockheed Martin** adapted a military concept called the kill chain for the cybersecurity industry, renaming it the **cyber kill chain**. This model identifies the stages of a cyberattack, giving defenders valuable insight into the typical tactics and techniques used by attackers during each phase. Like the traditional kill chain, the cyber kill chain is linear, assuming that attackers follow each stage sequentially.

Since its introduction, cyberattackers have evolved their tactics, often skipping certain stages of the kill chain. To address this, the security industry has adapted and developed new models. One such model is the **[MITRE ATT&CK® matrix](https://attack.mitre.org/)**, a detailed list of tactics and techniques based on real-world attacks. While it uses similar stages to the cyber kill chain, it doesn't adhere to a fixed, linear order.

In 2017, **Paul Pols**, in collaboration with **Fox-IT** and **Leiden University**, developed the **[Unified Kill Chain](https://www.unifiedkillchain.com/)**, which combines elements of both the MITRE ATT&CK matrix and the cyber kill chain, offering a more comprehensive model with **18 stages**.

## **Stages**

The cyber kill chain outlines a sequence of cyberattack phases that provide insight into the mindset of cyberattackers. It helps defenders understand attacker motives, methods, and techniques while identifying opportunities to thwart attacks early.

### **1. Reconnaissance**

In this phase, cyberattackers gather intelligence about the target, identifying weaknesses and vulnerabilities that can be exploited later.

### **2. Weaponization**

Bad actors use the information collected in the reconnaissance phase to create or modify [`malware`](/the-lab/core-principles/malware) tailored to exploit the weaknesses of the targeted organization.

### **3. Delivery**

Cyberattackers launch their attack by delivering the malware. Common methods include phishing or exploiting public, insecure wireless networks.

### **4. Exploitation**

After gaining access, cyberattackers move laterally through the organization's network, searching for sensitive data, additional vulnerabilities, or privileged accounts that can amplify the attack.

### **5. Installation**

At this stage, attackers install malware on multiple systems to maintain control over the compromised environment.

### **6. Command and Control (C2)**

Cyberattackers establish a remote control center, enabling them to operate their attack remotely. They often use obfuscation techniques to cover their tracks.

### **7. Actions on Objectives**

At this point, attackers execute their primary goal, which could include data exfiltration, encryption (ransomware), or system destruction.

### **8. Monetization**

Some cybersecurity experts have expanded the original cyber kill chain to include monetization activities such as demanding ransomware payments or selling sensitive data on the dark web.

## **Impact**

Understanding the cyber kill chain helps cybersecurity professionals identify threats early and implement effective defense strategies. By knowing the typical phases of an attack, defenders can mitigate vulnerabilities and respond proactively to potential threats.

## **Benefits**

The cyber kill chain offers several benefits for security professionals:

- **Identify threats** at every stage of the cyber kill chain.
- **Harden** privileged accounts, data, and systems.
- **Patch and upgrade** outdated hardware and software regularly.
- **Teach employees** how to spot phishing emails.
- **Respond quickly** to lateral movement within the network.
- **Stop in-progress cyberattacks** before they escalate.

## **Putting it into Practice**

To defend against cyberattacks, security professionals must deploy various tools and strategies that align with the cyber kill chain model. These include:

### **1. Threat Intelligence**

Good threat intelligence tools gather and synthesize data to provide actionable insights. These insights help security professionals detect attacks early.

### **2. IAM**

[Identity and Access Management ](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/security-101/what-is-identity-access-management-iam) solutions can detect anomalous activity, making it harder for attackers to escalate privileges or use stolen credentials. Multi-factor authentication-- [MFA](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/security-101/what-is-two-factor-authentication-2fa) adds an extra layer of security.

### **3. SIEM**

Security Information and Event Management tools aggregate data across the organization, surfacing critical cyber threats for security teams to address. They can even automate responses to certain threats, reducing the number of incidents to investigate.

### **4. EDR**

[Endpoint Detection and ResponseR](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/security-101/what-is-edr-endpoint-detection-response) solutions help monitor endpoints such as servers, computers, mobile devices, and IoT devices. Since bad actors often start attacks via compromised endpoints, monitoring these devices is critical for early detection.

### **5. XDR**

[Extended Detection and Response](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/security-101/what-is-xdr) solutions expand upon EDR, offering protection across endpoints, identities, cloud applications, and emails in a unified solution.

### **6. MDR**

[Managed Detection and Response](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/security-101/what-is-mdr-managed-detection-response) services help organizations without in-house security teams by providing 24/7 monitoring and threat response.

## **Common Challenges**

While the cyber kill chain is an invaluable tool for detecting and mitigating cyber threats, it has some limitations:

### **1. Focused on Malware**

The cyber kill chain was originally designed to detect and mitigate malware, but it’s less effective against attacks that don’t involve malware, such as attacks using compromised credentials.

### **2. Ideal for Perimeter Security**

The kill chain was more effective when there was a single network perimeter to defend. Today, with remote workers and cloud environments, monitoring every endpoint becomes more complex.

### **3. Not Equipped for Insider Threats**

The kill chain focuses on external threats and may struggle to detect insider threats, where the attacker already has some level of system access.

### **4. Too Linear**

Many cyberattacks deviate from the kill chain's linear process. Attackers may skip or combine several stages, which means focusing solely on the kill chain might leave gaps in defense.

## **Solutions**

As technology and cyberthreats evolve, the cyber kill chain must be complemented with additional solutions to cover emerging risks:

- **[AI-powered solutions](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/solutions/generative-ai-cybersecurity)** help detect and respond to advanced threats, including those powered by artificial intelligence.
- **Microsoft Security** offers a [unified SecOps platform](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/solutions/ai-powered-unified-secops-platform) that integrates **XDR** and **SIEM**, providing a holistic defense across all stages of the kill chain.
- Organizations are investing in AI-powered cybersecurity tools, like **[Microsoft Security Copilot](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/business/ai-machine-learning/microsoft-security-copilot)**, to combat evolving threats.
